# Five interpretations

_Five standalone readings drawn from the project. Each stands on its own — read
any one cold. With n = 8 countries and composition-only data, take each as a
plausible reading, pending more cities and cleaner coverage. Full numbers in
[RESIDUAL.md](./RESIDUAL.md) and [RESEARCH_LINK.md](./RESEARCH_LINK.md)._

---

## 1. A retail floor encodes the rent-versus-capability split GDP conceals

The project estimates a country's affluence from its shopping-mall directories,
then subtracts whatever its export complexity (ECI) can explain. For Australia the
leftover runs large and positive: a retail surface stocked well above its export
complexity, on one of the rich world's lowest ECI scores (−0.82) and the sample's
highest resource rents (13.4% of GDP). The method reconstructed a mining-rent
economy with no mining data in hand. GDP-per-capita would miss the split, since a
rentier and a complex economy can post the same income. Luxury-import composition
on the shelves carries the signal by itself.

## 2. The consumption side works as a lie-detector on export complexity

Export complexity (ECI) scores what a country makes for the world. The Philippines
scores high — 0.92, above Indonesia and Vietnam — yet its mall shelves read poor
and lean on remittances worth 8.7% of GDP. The gap points to borrowed complexity:
electronics assembly inside global value chains, with the headline score resting
on imported inputs and foreign design. A consumption estimate netted against ECI
exposes the mismatch where a trade statistic alone would wave it through. Owned
complexity tends to surface on local shelves; assembled complexity often leaves
them bare.

## 3. Resource rents and roadmap over-reach are one phenomenon seen twice

Two economies whose shelves look richer than their export complexity — Indonesia
and Malaysia — also carry the region's most aggressive resource-downstreaming
roadmaps: nickel into EV batteries, semiconductor entry. Resource rents do double
duty here. They open the affluence-without-capability gap, and they bankroll the
industrial policy aimed at closing it. Indonesia draws the extreme, pairing the
thinnest revealed tech base in the set with the widest gap between roadmap
ambition and current capability. The same signal points forward — locating where
strategy becomes a high-variance attempt to convert rents into capability
(Indonesia) versus a steadier consolidation on an existing base (Malaysia,
building on real packaging industry).

## 4. "Aligned" reports coherence and sits orthogonal to income level

When a country's consumption estimate and its export complexity land in the same
place, the residual reads near zero — call it aligned. Thailand and Vietnam both
sit there for different reasons: Thailand mature, Vietnam climbing on a rising ECI
and a zero ambition gap. Alignment reports the source and coherence of affluence
while leaving its level aside. A poor country can read aligned by earning what it
makes; a rich one can read misaligned, like Australia. The result reframes
development onto a 2×2 — how much a place consumes, set against how far its
production justifies the consumption.

## 5. The instrument is structurally blind to the remittance road

Luxury consumption is easy to scrape: flagship malls, branded tenants, clean
store-locator data. Remittance-funded mass consumption stays hard to capture —
name-only sari-sari shops, informal retail, uncaptured generic tenants. So the
Philippines' 8.7%-of-GDP remittance economy fades from a mall-directory dataset.
The data-generating process ends up mirroring the economic asymmetry under study.
Elite and import consumption surfaces cleanly; everyday domestic consumption
disappears. The instrument tilts toward the rentier-and-elite road and away from
the remittance-and-mass road, which raises a question past coverage — whose
consumption the digital surface of an economy records at all.
